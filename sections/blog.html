Why I grind meteorite analogues instead of dissolving them

Most prebiotic chemistry starts in water. But impacts, accretion and atmospheric entry are dry, violent, mechanical events — so what happens to organic molecules when the energy comes from force rather than solvent?

When we picture the origin of life, we usually imagine a warm little pond. Yet a large fraction of the organic inventory delivered to the early Earth travelled inside asteroids and comets, and arrived through processes that are anything but gentle: collisions during parent-body formation, shock from impacts, and the searing deceleration of atmospheric entry.

Mechanochemistry lets me reproduce that in the lab — driving reactions with mechanical force instead of heat or solvent. Grinding ribonucleosides with the metals and carbonates found in meteorites degrades them into exactly the nucleobases we detect in real samples. That hints the molecules we find today may be the survivors of a mechanical history, not pristine relics.
